Why precision is not just about speed

Mixed precision training is often described as a performance optimization — use half-precision floats, get 2× memory reduction and 8× compute speedup on Tensor Cores. That framing is incomplete. The reason mixed precision is subtle, and the reason engineers get burned by it, is that precision is also a numerical correctness question. Use the wrong format in the wrong place and your loss diverges silently, your gradients become NaN, or your model trains to a worse optimum without ever crashing.

The engineers who get mixed precision right understand the bit representation of each format well enough to know which values are representable, which operations require precision, and exactly what torch.autocast() casts. They don't add fp16=True as a magic flag — they know what that flag does at every layer of the computation.

This lesson builds that understanding from the bit level up to the PyTorch API. By the end, you will be able to look at any training crash involving NaN or Inf gradients and immediately know which component is responsible and why.

Floating-point formats at the bit level

IEEE 754 floating-point numbers are stored in three fields: sign, exponent, and mantissa (also called significand). The sign is always 1 bit. The exponent determines the range — how large or small values can be. The mantissa determines precision — how finely values are spaced within that range.

The formula: value = (−1)^sign × 2^(exponent − bias) × (1 + mantissa). The leading 1 in (1 + mantissa) is implicit — stored bits only encode the fractional part. The bias is a constant that shifts the exponent to allow both positive and negative exponents from the same unsigned bit field: 127 for FP32, 15 for FP16, 127 for BF16.

Why FP16 overflows and how loss scaling fixes it

FP16's maximum finite value is 65,504. The problem surfaces during backpropagation. The chain rule multiplies gradients together through many layers. In early layers of a deep network, gradients can be very large (exploding) or very small (vanishing). The exploding case is lethal for FP16: a gradient of 100,000 overflows to Inf. Once Inf appears in the graph, it propagates through every subsequent operation. The optimizer step produces NaN parameters. Training crashes — or worse, silently produces nonsense.

Gradient overflow is not rare. It's the normal state of FP16 training without mitigation, especially early in training when the loss is large, or with high learning rates, or with architectures that have large activations. Every production FP16 training run requires loss scaling.

Loss scaling: the mechanism

The solution is elegant: multiply the scalar loss by a large constant (the loss scale, typically 216 = 65,536) before the backward pass. By the chain rule, this multiplies every gradient in the entire network by the same factor. Gradients that would have been dangerously small (0.001, near FP16's minimum subnormal of ~6×10-8) become safely representable (65.5). Before the optimizer step, divide all gradients by the same scale factor, restoring their true values. The optimizer update sees correct gradient magnitudes.

The dangerous case is the reverse: a gradient that would have been 1,000 becomes 65,536,000 after scaling — overflowing FP16. This overflow is deliberately detectable: check all gradients for Inf or NaN after the backward pass. If found, skip the optimizer step entirely (model parameters unchanged, training step wasted but harmless) and reduce the scale factor. If clean for many consecutive steps, increase the scale factor to keep gradients as large as possible without overflowing.

from torch.cuda.amp import GradScaler, autocast

scaler = GradScaler()   # starts with scale=65536

for batch in dataloader:
    optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)

    with autocast(device_type='cuda', dtype=torch.float16):
        output = model(batch)
        loss   = criterion(output, labels)

    # scaled_loss = loss × scale → backward with inflated gradients
    scaler.scale(loss).backward()

    # unscales grads (÷scale), checks for inf/nan, calls optimizer if clean
    scaler.step(optimizer)

    # adjusts scale factor up or down
    scaler.update()

# Inspect: is the scale tracking correctly?
print(f"Current scale: {scaler.get_scale():.0f}")
# Tip: if scale drops to 1.0, your LR is too high. If it stays near 65536, everything is fine.

Why BF16 doesn't need loss scaling

The answer is entirely in the exponent field. BF16 has 8 exponent bits — identical to FP32. Same bias (127). Same representable exponent range. The BF16 maximum finite value is approximately 3.4×1038, exactly the same as FP32. A gradient would need to exceed 1038 to overflow a BF16 accumulator. This does not happen in any neural network training scenario.

BF16 is conceptually "FP32 with the bottom 16 mantissa bits discarded." Converting FP32 to BF16 is a bit-shift: keep the upper 16 bits, drop the lower 16. No exponent conversion. This is one reason BF16 hardware is cheap to implement — the exponent logic is reused from FP32 units. When you load a BF16 weight into an FP32 accumulator, the exponent field just slots directly into position.

The cost is precision: BF16's 7 mantissa bits represent values with about 0.8% relative error (1/128). FP32's 23-bit mantissa gives 0.000006% error. For neural network forward passes — matrix multiplications, activations — this precision loss is empirically harmless. Thousands of training runs at all scales have confirmed that BF16 converges to the same optima as FP32 training. The rounding errors are random and average out. For the optimizer step, however, precision matters critically: you're adding a very small gradient-scaled update (maybe 0.000001) to a parameter value (maybe 1.3). BF16's 0.8% error would corrupt this update entirely. This is why optimizer master weights always stay in FP32.

The rule in one sentence

Use BF16 on Ampere and newer (A100, A10, RTX 3090/4090, H100). Use FP16 + GradScaler on Turing and Volta (T4, V100). BF16 is hardware-supported on Ampere as of 2020; on Turing GPUs it falls back to software emulation and provides no speedup. If you're using the free Colab T4, use FP16 with loss scaling.

What torch.autocast() actually does

torch.autocast() does not cast all operations to half precision. This is the most common misconception. It selectively casts only the operations that are (a) numerically stable at lower precision and (b) accelerated by Tensor Cores. Everything else stays in its current dtype. The decisions are baked into an internal op-level policy table.

Operations Autocast decision Why
matmul, linear, conv2d, bmm → BF16 / FP16 Tensor Core acceleration; numerically stable for large dot products
relu, gelu, silu, elementwise mul/add → BF16 / FP16 Elementwise ops stable at half precision
layer_norm, group_norm, softmax, log_softmax stay FP32 Need precision: softmax computes exp() of potentially large values; LayerNorm computes variance requiring precision
cross_entropy, nll_loss, mse_loss stay FP32 Accumulation of per-element errors requires full precision
Model parameters (.weight, .bias) never touched Autocast creates temporary lower-precision copies for each op but never modifies the stored parameter tensors

The last row is critical: autocast does not modify your model's parameters. For each matmul, it creates a temporary BF16 copy of the weight, runs the matmul in BF16, then discards the copy. The model's .weight tensor remains in FP32. This is different from model.to(torch.bfloat16), which permanently converts the weights. Autocast is a per-operation casting context. Model conversion is a permanent dtype change.

import torch, torch.nn as nn

model = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(512, 512),
    nn.LayerNorm(512),
    nn.GELU(),
    nn.Linear(512, 512),
).cuda()

x = torch.randn(4, 512, device='cuda')  # FP32 input

def dtype_hook(name):
    def hook(m, inp, out):
        in_dt  = inp[0].dtype
        out_dt = out.dtype
        print(f"  {name}: input={in_dt}, output={out_dt}")
    return hook

model[0].register_forward_hook(dtype_hook("Linear1"))
model[1].register_forward_hook(dtype_hook("LayerNorm"))
model[2].register_forward_hook(dtype_hook("GELU"))
model[3].register_forward_hook(dtype_hook("Linear2"))

print("--- With autocast(bfloat16) ---")
with torch.autocast(device_type='cuda', dtype=torch.bfloat16):
    y = model(x)
# Expected output:
#   Linear1:   input=float32,  output=bfloat16   ← cast for Tensor Core
#   LayerNorm: input=bfloat16, output=float32    ← upcast for precision
#   GELU:      input=float32,  output=float32    ← follows LayerNorm output
#   Linear2:   input=float32,  output=bfloat16   ← cast again for Tensor Core

# Weight dtype is unchanged:
print(f"model[0].weight.dtype = {model[0].weight.dtype}")  # float32
LayerNorm output is float32 even with BF16 autocast

This surprises engineers: your activations are BF16, they go into LayerNorm, and LayerNorm outputs FP32. This is the autocast policy in action. The output of LayerNorm then goes into the next Linear, which autocast casts back to BF16. So your model alternates between BF16 (matmuls) and FP32 (norms) at every layer. This is the correct behavior — it's preserving precision exactly where precision matters. The extra cast overhead is small compared to the Tensor Core speedup from running matmuls in BF16.

Tensor Cores: the hardware that makes this matter

Without Tensor Cores, mixed precision would give memory savings but no compute speedup — interesting but not transformative. Tensor Cores make the compute speedup real and large.

Tensor Cores are dedicated hardware units inside Ampere/Hopper SMs that perform matrix multiply-accumulate operations in a single clock cycle. A Tensor Core takes two 4×4 matrices (in BF16/FP16) and adds their product to a 4×4 accumulator (in FP32) in one cycle. Effectively: a complete 4×4 matrix multiplication per clock, with FP32 accumulation for precision. Multiple Tensor Cores run per SM, across all 108 SMs, at whatever the GPU's boost clock is.

Tensor Cores have one strict requirement that trips up custom architectures: matrix dimensions must be multiples of 8 for BF16/FP16, and multiples of 16 for INT8. If your vocabulary embedding size is 50,257 (like GPT-2) or your hidden dimension is 768 (an old standard) — the matmul might still use Tensor Cores (PyTorch pads internally in some cases), but you cannot guarantee it. Modern production architectures explicitly pad dimensions to multiples of 64 (the Tensor Core warp tile size) to guarantee maximum utilization. If you design a custom architecture and matmuls seem slower than expected, check alignment first.

FP8: the frontier

FP8 is available on H100 and Ada Lovelace GPUs. Two variants exist:

E4M3 (4 exponent bits, 3 mantissa bits): maximum value ±448. Used for weights and activations in the forward pass, where you want more precision within a limited range.

E5M2 (5 exponent bits, 2 mantissa bits): maximum value ±57,344. Used for gradients in the backward pass, where the larger range is more important than precision.

FP8 delivers approximately 2× the throughput of BF16 on H100 Tensor Cores: ~2,000 TFLOP/s vs ~1,000 TFLOP/s. The challenge is that FP8's tiny range (max 448 for E4M3) requires an explicit scale factor per tensor. Unlike BF16 — where the range is so large that scaling is never needed — each FP8 tensor needs a scale value that maps its actual dynamic range into [−448, 448]. Scale factors must be updated every step as gradient magnitudes evolve. This overhead is manageable in frameworks like NVIDIA's TransformerEngine but is not yet handled automatically by PyTorch's autocast.

For training today: if you're on H100, investigate FP8 via TransformerEngine. For everything else, BF16 is the right default and will remain so for several more years of typical hardware access.

One common mistake: HuggingFace's from_pretrained defaults to FP32 unless you specify torch_dtype. Loading Llama-3-8B in FP32 on an A100 uses 32 GB instead of 16 GB — over the budget if you're adding optimizer state. Always specify:

# Correct: load in BF16, train with autocast
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    "meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-8B",
    torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
    device_map="auto",
)
model.config.use_cache = False

# BF16 training loop — no GradScaler
for batch in dataloader:
    optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)
    with torch.autocast(device_type='cuda', dtype=torch.bfloat16):
        loss = model(**batch).loss
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

# FP16 training loop for T4 — GradScaler required
scaler = torch.cuda.amp.GradScaler()
for batch in dataloader:
    optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)
    with torch.autocast(device_type='cuda', dtype=torch.float16):
        loss = model(**batch).loss
    scaler.scale(loss).backward()
    scaler.step(optimizer)
    scaler.update()

Exercise 1: Inspect the bit representation of FP32, FP16, BF16. Write a function that extracts the sign, exponent, and mantissa fields for each format and prints them. Then verify the key numbers: FP16 max (65504), BF16 max (same as FP32), FP16 precision loss for a value like 1.001.

import struct, numpy as np, torch

def bits32(x):
    b = struct.unpack('I', struct.pack('f', x))[0]
    print(f"FP32({x:.6f}): S={b>>31} E={(b>>23)&0xFF}-127={(b>>23&0xFF)-127} M={b&0x7FFFFF:023b}")

def bits16(x):
    v = np.float16(x)
    b = np.frombuffer(v.tobytes(), dtype=np.uint16)[0]
    print(f"FP16({x}): S={b>>15} E={(b>>10)&0x1F}-15={(b>>10&0x1F)-15} M={b&0x3FF:010b} → {float(v):.6f}")

bits32(1.001)     # high precision: 1.001000
bits16(1.001)     # low precision: rounds to 1.000977
bits16(65504.0)   # FP16 max
bits16(65536.0)   # overflow: Inf

# BF16 via torch (numpy doesn't have bfloat16)
bf = torch.tensor(1.001).to(torch.bfloat16)
print(f"BF16(1.001) → {float(bf):.6f}")  # rounds to 1.000000 (only ~2 digits)
bf_big = torch.tensor(3.4e38).to(torch.bfloat16)
print(f"BF16(3.4e38) → {float(bf_big):.2e}")  # still representable, no overflow

Exercise 2: Deliberately trigger a GradScaler skip. Train GPT-2 with FP16 and a very large learning rate (1.0). Print scaler.get_scale() after each step. Watch it drop by 2× every time Inf gradients appear. Then reduce the LR and watch recovery.

model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("gpt2").cuda()
optim  = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1.0)  # intentionally huge
scaler = torch.cuda.amp.GradScaler()
x = torch.randint(0, 50257, (2, 256), device='cuda')

for step in range(15):
    optim.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)
    with torch.autocast('cuda', dtype=torch.float16):
        loss = model(x, labels=x).loss
    scaler.scale(loss).backward()
    scaler.step(optim)
    scaler.update()
    print(f"step {step:2d}: loss={loss.item():.3f}, scale={scaler.get_scale():.0f}")

What comes next

You now understand the full precision picture: why FP16 overflows (5-bit exponent, max 65,504), how GradScaler detects and recovers, why BF16 never needs loss scaling (8-bit exponent, same range as FP32, at the cost of precision that training doesn't need), what autocast actually casts (matmuls yes, norms no, optimizer state never), and where FP8 fits (H100 training frontier). These are the tools you need to diagnose any precision-related training failure.

Lesson 05 moves from training memory to inference memory: the KV cache. You've seen that training memory is dominated by activations and optimizer state. During inference those disappear — but a new term appears that grows with every token generated. At 128K context, the KV cache for a single Llama-3-8B request is larger than the model itself. Understanding its formula, and the innovations that tame it (GQA, paged attention), is what separates engineers who can deploy LLMs efficiently from those who can only train them.
